Chinese Festivals

Lunar New Year and Lantern Festival shine with tradition, family, and hopes for a bright year ahead.

Lunar New Year (spring festival)

The Chinese Lunar New Year, also known as the Spring Festival, is the most important traditional celebration in China, marking the beginning of the lunar calendar. Its origins trace back over 4,000 years to ancient harvest festivals and legends like that of the beast **Nian**, which was scared away by red decorations, loud firecrackers, and bright lights. Today, it is celebrated with family reunions, giving red envelopes (hongbao), decorating homes with red lanterns and couplets, and enjoying feasts. The festival lasts for 15 days, ending with the Lantern Festival, symbolizing unity, renewal, and good fortune for the year ahead.

First Lunar New Year (Chinese New Year)

Date: The Lunar New Year tradition dates back over 4,000 years, so approximately 2000 BCE or earlier in ancient China.
Place: Ancient China, with roots traced to the Shang Dynasty and possibly earlier agrarian societies.
Details: The festival started as a way to mark the end of winter and beginning of spring, related to agricultural cycles and folk beliefs.

Lantern Festival

The Lantern Festival, celebrated on the 15th day of the Chinese Lunar New Year, marks the end of the Spring Festival and the first full moon of the new year. It originated over 2,000 years ago during the Han Dynasty and is rooted in Buddhist traditions of lighting lanterns to honor the Buddha. Over time, it evolved into a joyful cultural celebration. People hang colorful lanterns, solve riddles written on them, and enjoy sweet glutinous rice balls called **tangyuan**, symbolizing family unity and wholeness. The night is lit with parades, dragon dances, and fireworks, creating a magical and festive atmosphere.
