Diwali (festival of lights)

Diwali, also known as the Festival of Lights, is one of the most important and widely celebrated festivals in India. It symbolizes the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. Rooted in Hindu mythology, Diwali commemorates Lord Ramaâ€™s return to Ayodhya after 14 years of exile and his victory over the demon king Ravana. Homes are decorated with oil lamps (diyas), colorful rangoli, and lights. Families come together to worship the goddess Lakshmi, the deity of wealth and prosperity, and celebrate with fireworks, sweets, and the exchange of gifts, creating a vibrant and joyous atmosphere.

Holi Festival

Holi, known as the Festival of Colors, is a joyous Hindu celebration that marks the arrival of spring and the triumph of good over evil. Rooted in ancient legends, it commemorates the story of Prahlad and Holika, symbolizing devotion and the power of faith. Holi begins with a bonfire called Holika Dahan, signifying the burning away of negativity. The next day, people take to the streets to throw vibrant colored powders, dance, and sing. First Holi

Date: Holi's earliest mentions date back to ancient Hindu texts, around 3000 years ago.
Place: Ancient India, with significant cultural roots in the Braj region (around Mathura and Vrindavan).
Details: Holi, the Festival of Colors, celebrates the triumph of good over evil and the arrival of spring. It is linked to the legend of Prahlad and Holika, where divine protection saved Prahlad from evil forces. The festival is known for vibrant color throwing, singing, dancing, and community gatherings.
