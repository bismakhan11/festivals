Islamic Festivals

Celebrating the two most joyful occasions in the Islamic calendar: Eid-ul-Fitr and Eid-ul-Adha

Eid-ul-Fitr

Ramadan, the ninth month of the Islamic calendar, is a sacred time of fasting, prayer, reflection, and spiritual growth for Muslims around the world. It commemorates the month in which the Qur'an was first revealed to the Prophet Muhammad (PBUH), and believers fast from dawn until sunset, abstaining from food, drink, and other physical needs to purify the soul and develop empathy for those in need. The fast is broken daily with a meal called Iftar, often shared with family and community. As Ramadan comes to an end after 29 or 30 days, Muslims celebrate Eid-ul-Fitr, the Festival of Breaking the Fast. This joyful occasion begins with a special prayer at the mosque, followed by festive meals, gift-giving, wearing new clothes, and acts of charity such as Zakat al-Fitr. Eid-ul-Fitr is not only a celebration of the end of fasting, but also a reward for the spiritual discipline and devotion shown throughout Ramadan.

First Eid ul-Fitr

Date: The first Eid al-Fitr was celebrated in 624 CE (1 AH in the Islamic calendar)
Place: Medina, in present-day Saudi Arabia.
Details: Eid al-Fitr marks the end of Ramadan, the month of fasting. The Prophet Muhammad and his followers celebrated the first Eid al-Fitr after their first Ramadan fasting in Medina. This established the tradition of Eid as a day of thanksgiving and communal prayer.

Eid-ul-Adha

Eid-ul-Adha, also known as the Festival of Sacrifice, is one of the most significant Islamic holidays, observed by Muslims worldwide. It honors the unwavering faith and obedience of Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham), who was willing to sacrifice his son Ismail (Ishmael) as an act of devotion to Allah. Before the sacrifice could take place, Allah intervened and provided a ram to be sacrificed instead.
